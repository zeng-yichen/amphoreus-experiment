This is worth paying attention to — and not just because of the milestone.

Here's why.

When your patient population has already failed existing treatments, your effect size estimation gets complicated fast. The published literature on ulcerative colitis gives you one set of expected treatment responses. But those numbers are based on broader populations — not the biologic-refractory subset. Your Phase 1 data gives you a different signal based on your specific mechanism of action. And if your AI platform is generating multi-omic biomarker data on top of that, you now have three distinct sources of evidence that may not agree with each other.

Combining those into a defensible sample size calculation for Phase 2 is where trials either set themselves up for a clean Phase 3 — or generate 18 months of data the FDA can't act on.

The critical question at this stage isn't whether the AI identified the right target. It's whether the clinical endpoints being measured in Phase 2 will translate into what the FDA requires for a pivotal Phase 3 submission.

The science is brilliant. The regulatory pathway doesn't support it.

The fix is straightforward but requires early planning: identify the validated functional outcome measures the FDA has accepted for similar indications. Make those your primary endpoints. Keep the AI-generated biomarker data as supportive secondary endpoints that strengthen your mechanistic story without carrying the regulatory burden.
