In the last 90 days, we've worked with a number of companies developing trials for interesting diseases and one had an interesting problem:

There may only be several hundred people in America with this disease. The FDA will grant approval based on probable benefit to get the device on the market anyway. No randomized controlled trial. No statistical demonstration of efficacy. Just a handful of case studies showing "no serious problems occurred." How? The Humanitarian Device Exemption pathway.

Here's the impossible math: Traditional clinical trials should have ~80% power to detect a clinically meaningful difference. For most devices, that's 50+ patients minimum. But what if only 100 people per year in the entire US have the disease? You cannot recruit enough patients for a well-powered study.

The FDA's solution is the HDE. For diseases affecting fewer than 8,000 patients annually, the statistical requirements fundamentally change.

Traditional IDE pathway: Pilot studies establishing safety/efficacy ranges. Power calculations with expected effect sizes. Statistical hypothesis testing vs. control.

HDE pathway: whatever limited patient data they have demonstrating basic safety. Release device to market under study conditions. Monitor ALL patients who use it. Report issues as they emerge.

We recently worked on both pathways simultaneously. Same client. Similar interventions. Two different patient populations. 

Larger population (traditional IDE): Rigorous pilot data with well-powered sample sizes and explicit statistical analysis plan. 

For our smaller population (HDE): Extremely limited pilot data. Limited sample sizes with statistical analyses limited by availability of data. Applying for the HDE was along the lines of: "Here are two patients who seem fine so far. Let's carefully monitor everyone else."

The HDE isn't a shortcut - it’s a trade. You trade the front-end burden of a  randomized trial for a back-end burden of mandatory IRB oversight and perpetual surveillance.
