I never thought data labeling would humble me. But it did, badly.

At the time, I was CRO at Appen. We'd landed a seven-figure contract where one of the FAANG companies needed a million prompt-response pairs labeled (within 72 hours). 

We couldn't get enough people, so I rolled up my sleeves and spent the weekend data labeling.

I figured: I'm reasonably educated, how hard could this be?

Holy cr*p.

Within the hour, I quickly realized how difficult a "simple task" of data labeling was. The 12 page pdf set of instructions felt like reading raw Kubernetes config files.

So I called the PM: "Who wrote these?"

"The client."

I knew right then: if I couldn't execute this, we certainly wouldn't be able to do it at scale with any real level of quality either.

Although those 72 hours were hell, I’ll always be grateful for that epiphany.

This led me to meet Alan Cowen. He taught me everything around semantic space theory. In simpler terms, every one of our emotions has a precise coordinate on a map. Plot it, and you can automate it.

This was a huge unlock for data infrastructure for voice AI, since voice only works in if it best reflects human emotions on the backend:

• Data sets could now contain hundreds of emotional labels 
• Enabling exponential improvement of data infrastructure behind voice models
• All while leveraging humans in the loop to drive maximum quality data sets 

This was the goldmine.

He figured out that if he spent years head down building a methodology so deep that no other companies could replicate it, they would have no choice but to buy it from the infrastructure expert themselves.
