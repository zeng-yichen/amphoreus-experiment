One of the most flattering things I've ever been told:


"You smell like an engineer."


When my mentor hired me at Greenplum in 2009, I was good at sales. Building relationships, finding pain points, closing deals. That was my world.


But Greenplum was a deep-tech database company. And on day one, my mentor showed me our "product."


It was a piece of paper with a bunch of code on it: database kernels, distributed architecture, query optimization. I looked at it, then at him, then back at the paper. I had absolutely no idea what I was supposed to sell.


I was alone in a new role, staring at symbols I didn't understand, knowing I'd need to earn the attention of CIOs to sign million-dollar contracts.


So I figured that if I wanted to sell to technical buyers, I needed to speak their language. I was going to learn this until I was blue in the face.


I started with in database parallelized algorithms, cost based optimizers, and scale out x86 architectures, then worked my way into decision trees and Map Reduce. 


Things that previously made my eyes glaze over became my nightly homework. I studied after hours, asked engineers embarrassing questions, and sat in on technical calls just to absorb the vocabulary.


The payoff came slowly, then all at once.


I was on a sales call with a team of developers when one of them stopped mid-sentence, looked at me, and said it:


"You look like a salesperson, but you smell like an engineer."


They liked hanging with me because I could communicate in technical terms. I wasn't translating their work into business-speak, but by speaking their language.


That's when I realized: when a salesperson becomes technical, the bridge between sales and engineering gets smaller. It helped close deals faster, build rapport, and win bigger contracts.


I took two major lessons from that journey:


1) If you find yourself underqualified for the room you're in, face the gap honestly, commit to the learning, put in the reps nightly, and let the validation come naturally.


2) Technical buyers can "smell" authenticity from miles away. I am not saying you need to become an engineer, but you need to respect their world enough to meet them in it.


The biggest career superpower I'd recommend to any salesperson: learn the tech as well as an engineer would.


The conversations get better, trust gets stronger, and occasionally you get engineer praise (in the form of a backhanded compliment).
