“We’ll just build it ourselves.” - almost every biopharma CIO/CTO before burning 12+ months and $1M+ on an AI tool that never shipped.

If you’re a biopharma in 2026, there’s one thing you have to know: the bigger your organization, the more likely you’ll try and build an internal clinical AI tool before you consider options. The reality is, these builds are also more likely to fail. Here’s why:

I've seen this pattern repeatedly: enterprise biopharma teams with large budgets and genuine intent decide they're going to build an internal solution for clinical operations. Monitoring intelligence, site collaboration, the next set of dashboards to solve the n+1 visibility issue, whatever the use case. They assemble a team, allocate a budget, and start building.

12-18 months later, many projects are either dead, "still in experimentation," or have produced something that technically works but cannot be used at scale.

The failure mode isn't just technical. It's more fundamental.

Here's what goes wrong:

The teams are often contractor-heavy with dispersed knowledge. You get strong individual engineers, but they don't have deep clinical domain expertise. They understand APIs and model architectures, but not how exactly a protocol needs to align with the organization's SOPs, internal templates, and prior study data - or why a CRF alignment issue in Week 3 cascades into a 2-month delay by Week 12. Domain knowledge isn't something you can sprint on. It takes years of working inside clinical operations to understand the edge cases that break systems at scale.

Getting access to the right data takes longer than building the tool. Scaling development to account for different priorities, therapeutic areas, and use cases means you're not building one tool; you're effectively building a combination, each with its own data model and validation requirements.

Internal approvals slow everything to a crawl. Compliance review, security assessment, IT architecture review, stakeholder alignment. Every step that would take a vendor two weeks takes an internal team two months because there are more sign-offs, competing priorities, and knowledge silos.

Meanwhile, purpose-built vendors are solving these exact problems as their entire focus. They're laser-focused on that singular mission, they iterate faster, and they've already incorporated the precise combination of domain and technical expertise.

So when should you build vs. buy?

Build when: You have a massive proprietary data advantage that no vendor can replicate or feasibly integrate, your use case is so niche that no platform will fit, and you have dedicated engineering resources with personal clinical domain expertise.

Buy when: The problem is well-defined across the industry (e.g. protocol authoring, monitoring agents), efficiency matters more than deep customization, and your internal team's time is better spent running trials than maintaining tools, orchestration, and infrastructure.
